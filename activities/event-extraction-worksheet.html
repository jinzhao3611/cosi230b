Background

You are annotating data for an AI system that extracts events from news text.

An event consists of:

  • A trigger (the word or phrase that signals the event)
  • One or more arguments (participants, locations, times, etc.)
Event Type: ATTACK
An action involving physical or violent harm against a person, group, or location.

Annotation Labels

Trigger labels:

B-ATTACK beginning of attack trigger
I-ATTACK continuation of trigger
O not part of a trigger

Argument labels:

B-AGENT / I-AGENT who carried out the attack
B-TARGET / I-TARGET who/what was attacked
B-PLACE / I-PLACE where it happened

Key Takeaway

Sequence labeling becomes hard when labels depend on interpretation, not just word identity.

Event extraction requires:

  • Semantic understanding
  • Consistent guidelines
  • Acceptance of disagreement

Part X: Aspectual Verbs and Event Boundaries (Hard Case)

Aspectual words describe the beginning, continuation, or end of an event, rather than the event itself.

Common aspectual words: began, started, continued, stopped, resumed, ended

Whether these should be included as part of an event trigger is often unclear and guideline-dependent.

Key Teaching Insight

Aspectual words force annotators to decide whether an event is something that happens, something that starts happening, or something that is ongoing.

This decision affects: trigger boundaries, event counts, and downstream temporal reasoning.

One-sentence takeaway: Event annotation requires deciding what counts as the event itself versus information about the event.

Final Reflection

Event Annotation Philosophy

Event annotation is not just labeling what happened — it is deciding what counts as having happened.

Aspectual words force annotators to draw a line between:

  • Events
  • Intentions
  • Temporal structure

That line directly shapes: the dataset, the model, and downstream conclusions.

One-Sentence Takeaway

Annotation guidelines encode a theory of events, whether we admit it or not.
